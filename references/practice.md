# Practice & Technical Feedback Reference

Load when user asks for practice plans, technique help, wants to improve something specific,
describes a problem with their playing, or needs exercises.

---

## DIAGNOSING TECHNICAL PROBLEMS

Before giving advice, identify the root cause. Most problems fall into 5 categories:

### 1. Tension & Physical Issues
**Symptoms**: pain, fatigue quickly, stiffness, cramps
**Root causes**: bad posture, grip too tight, poor hand position, no warm-up
**Fix approach**: address posture and relaxation FIRST before anything else

Signs of excessive tension:
- Shoulder raised while playing
- Wrist bent at extreme angles
- Thumb squeezing the neck (guitar)
- Fingers curling in toward palm when not pressing

### 2. Timing & Rhythm Issues
**Symptoms**: rushing, dragging, inconsistent tempo, losing time
**Root causes**: not using metronome, subdividing wrong, muscle memory without internalized beat
**Fix approach**: metronome work + conscious subdivision

### 3. Technique / Motor Control Issues
**Symptoms**: notes not clean, can't play fast, sloppy transitions
**Root causes**: practicing too fast, inefficient motion, bad fingering choices
**Fix approach**: slow down dramatically (50% of target speed), isolate the problem spot

### 4. Memory & Mental Issues
**Symptoms**: blanking out, inconsistent, works in practice but not performance
**Root causes**: not memorized deeply enough, only surface-level memory
**Fix approach**: practice in chunks, backward chaining, practice with distractions

### 5. Reading & Ear Issues
**Symptoms**: can't sight-read, can't play by ear, disconnect between sound and symbol
**Root causes**: insufficient ear training, lack of reading practice
**Fix approach**: daily short sight-reading + interval/chord recognition exercises

## THE FUNDAMENTAL RULE OF PRACTICE

> **Never practice mistakes.**

If you play something wrong 10 times, you've practiced the wrong version 10 times.
It is now in muscle memory. Fixing it takes 3-5x longer than learning it right.

**Corollary**: Always practice at a speed where you can play it correctly.
If you can't play it correctly at any speed, you haven't analyzed it enough.

## PRACTICE METHODS

### 1. Chunking
Break the piece into small sections (2-4 bars max).
Master each chunk before connecting them.
Connect in pairs: A+B, B+C, C+D, then A+B+C, B+C+D, then full.

### 2. Slow practice
The speed at which you practice is NOT the speed at which you perform.
Practice at 50-60% of target speed. Increase only when perfect at current speed.
Use a metronome. Every session.

### 3. Backward chaining
Learn the last chunk first, then add the second-to-last, etc.
Why: you always go from known to unknown. The ending never gets weak.

### 4. Rhythmic variation
Take a passage and practice with dotted rhythms, swing, or other alterations.
Example (sixteenth note run): practice as ♩♪ ♩♪ pattern, then ♪♩ ♪♩ pattern.
This builds finger independence and catches weak notes.

### 5. Mental practice
Sit away from your instrument and "play" through the piece in your head.
If you can't hear it clearly in your imagination, you don't know it well enough.

### 6. Distraction practice
Once you know a piece, practice while holding a conversation, watching TV, etc.
If it breaks down, you haven't fully automatized it yet.

### 7. Isolation
If a transition between two notes/positions is failing, isolate just those 2 notes.
Loop them until the transition is effortless, then re-integrate.

## GIVING FEEDBACK: THE SPECIFICITY RULE

**Bad feedback** (generic, useless):
- "Practice more slowly"
- "Work on your technique"
- "You need to practice more"

**Good feedback** (specific, actionable):
- "Set your metronome to 60 BPM. Play only measures 5-8, repeating until you can
  play 5 times in a row without mistakes. Then raise to 65 BPM."
- "Your third finger is collapsing at the first joint when you fret the D string.
  Practice just that note, focusing on keeping the finger curved."
- "The timing is rushing in the triplet passage (measure 12). Count aloud '1-trip-let
  2-trip-let' while you play it until it feels natural."

**Structure of good feedback**:
1. Name the exact problem (measure, note, technique)
2. Explain why it's happening (root cause)
3. Give one specific exercise or fix
4. Set a clear success criterion ("when you can do X, then move to Y")

## PRACTICE PLAN TEMPLATES

### Template A: 30-minute general practice
```
0:00 – 0:05  WARM-UP
             Scales or arpeggios at comfortable tempo
             Long tones (wind/string) or finger stretches (guitar/piano)

0:05 – 0:15  TECHNIQUE FOCUS
             One specific technique being developed this week
             Use methods: slow practice, isolation, rhythmic variation

0:15 – 0:25  REPERTOIRE
             Current piece(s) being learned
             Don't run through from start to finish — work problem spots

0:25 – 0:30  FREE PLAY / EAR TRAINING
             Improvise over a simple backing
             Or: interval/chord recognition exercise
```

### Template B: 60-minute dedicated practice
```
0:00 – 0:10  WARM-UP
             Physical warm-up + technical exercises

0:10 – 0:25  TECHNIQUE
             Focused drill on one specific skill
             With metronome, with BPM tracking

0:25 – 0:45  REPERTOIRE
             10 min: current difficult piece
             10 min: review of previously learned material

0:45 – 0:55  EAR TRAINING / THEORY
             Interval recognition, singing scales, transcribing a short phrase

0:55 – 1:00  FREE EXPLORATION
             Improvise, compose, experiment without judgment
```

### Template C: 15-minute micro-session (busy days)
```
0:00 – 0:03  Warm-up (just scales, no elaboration)
0:03 – 0:12  ONE THING: pick the hardest passage, slow practice only
0:12 – 0:15  Play something you already know well — end on a positive note
```

## BPM PROGRESSION SYSTEM

For technique-based goals:
1. Start at 60% of target tempo
2. Play perfectly 5 times in a row
3. Add 5 BPM
4. Repeat
5. When you reach target tempo, stay there for 2-3 sessions before moving on

**For difficult passages**: start at whatever tempo allows zero mistakes.
If that's 40 BPM, then 40 BPM it is.

## COMMON PROBLEMS & SPECIFIC FIXES

### Guitar: Can't play the F barre chord
**Root cause**: insufficient finger strength, or thumb position
**Fix**:
1. Place first finger across all 6 strings at fret 1, press hard, strum — aim for all 6 ringing
2. Practice the partial barre (strings 1-2 only) first
3. Check thumb: it should be behind the middle finger, not sticking out at the side
4. Practice just the barre shape for 2 min/day — takes 2-6 weeks typically

### Guitar: Picking hand inconsistency
**Root cause**: no anchor, inconsistent pick angle/depth
**Fix**: anchor your picking hand (rest pinky or heel on body), pick no deeper than 2mm

### Guitar/Bass: Moving between chords slowly
**Root cause**: lifting fingers too far from strings, moving one finger at a time
**Fix**: keep all fingers as close to strings as possible, visualize the target shape BEFORE moving

### Piano: Can't coordinate hands
**Root cause**: trying to learn both hands together before knowing each hand alone
**Fix**: learn RH alone to 100%, LH alone to 100%, then combine at 50% tempo

### Piano: Uneven tone/dynamics
**Root cause**: finger weight not controlled, relying only on finger velocity
**Fix**: practice with arm weight, not finger speed — slow forte practice

### Any instrument: Rushing (playing ahead of the beat)
**Root cause**: anticipating the next note before finishing the current one
**Fix**: set metronome to half tempo, accent the off-beats, think about sustaining notes

### Any instrument: Performance anxiety
**Root cause**: gap between practice conditions and performance conditions
**Fix**: introduce pressure gradually — record yourself, play for one person, then small groups

## EAR TRAINING EXERCISES

### Level 1 (Beginner)
- Sing the major scale every day (do-re-mi)
- Identify: is the second note higher or lower? (melodic direction)
- Recognize perfect octave and perfect 5th by association (5th = "Star Wars" opening)

### Level 2 (Intermediate)
- Identify all intervals (use songs as mnemonics: m2=Jaws, M2=Happy Birthday, M3=When the Saints, P4=Here Comes the Bride, tritone=The Simpsons, P5=Star Wars, M6=NBC chime, m7=Somewhere (West Side Story), M7=Take On Me)
- Sing chord arpeggios (major, minor, dominant 7th)
- Transcribe simple melodies by ear (nursery rhymes → pop melodies)

### Level 3 (Advanced)
- Identify chord quality by ear (major, minor, dominant, diminished, half-dim)
- Transcribe chord progressions (identify I, IV, V, vi by ear)
- Sing the guide tones of chords (3rd and 7th)
- Transcribe solos note-by-note

### Level 4 (Expert)
- Identify modes by their characteristic sound
- Transcribe complete jazz heads and solos
- Identify chord extensions by ear (9th, ♭9, ♯11, 13th)
