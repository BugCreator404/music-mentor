## SONG STRUCTURE

### Common forms

**Verse-Chorus (Pop/Rock)**
```
Intro → Verse → Pre-chorus → Chorus → Verse → Pre-chorus → Chorus → Bridge → Chorus → Outro
```
- Verse: tells the story, lower energy, melody varies
- Pre-chorus: builds tension toward chorus
- Chorus: emotional peak, repeated, hook lives here
- Bridge: contrast, new perspective, often modulates or changes texture

**ABA / Ternary (Classical / Jazz)**
```
A (statement) → B (contrast/development) → A (return, often varied)
```

**AABA (Jazz standard)**
```
A (8 bars) → A (8 bars, repeated) → B "bridge" (8 bars) → A (8 bars)
Total: 32 bars. Used in: "Autumn Leaves," "All The Things You Are"
```

**12-bar Blues**
```
I7 | I7 | I7 | I7
IV7| IV7| I7 | I7
V7 | IV7| I7 | V7
```

**Through-composed**: no repeating sections, narrative-driven (art songs, film music)

## MELODY WRITING

### Principles of good melody

1. **Contour**: Melodies need a shape — rising tension, falling resolution
   - Don't stay on the same pitch too long
   - Use stepwise motion (2nds) as the "spine", leaps (3rds+) for drama
   - Climax note: usually occurs 2/3 through the phrase

2. **Rhythm**: The rhythm of a melody often matters more than the notes
   - Syncopation creates energy and groove
   - Repeated rhythmic motifs create identity (think Beethoven's 5th: short-short-short-long)

3. **Range**: Most vocal melodies stay within a 10th (an octave + a 3rd)
   - Instrument range considerations: always know the comfortable range of your target instrument

4. **Phrase length**: 4-bar phrases are the norm (listeners expect it)
   - Varying phrase length creates surprise: a 5-bar phrase feels extended, 3-bar feels cut short

5. **Resolution**: Melodies that end on the tonic feel resolved, on the 5th feel open

### Motivic development techniques
Take a short motif (2-4 notes) and:
- **Sequence**: repeat starting on different pitches (up or down a step)
- **Inversion**: flip it upside down (ascending intervals become descending)
- **Retrograde**: play it backwards
- **Augmentation**: make the note values longer (twice as slow)
- **Diminution**: make the note values shorter (twice as fast)
- **Fragmentation**: use only part of the motif

## HARMONIC COMPOSITION TOOLKIT

### Starting a composition
1. Choose a key and mode → sets the emotional palette
2. Choose a tempo and time signature → sets the physical energy
3. Write a 4-bar chord progression → this is your harmonic foundation
4. Write a melody over it → test if the progression supports the emotional intent
5. Revise: often the melody reveals what the chords should actually be

### Making progressions more interesting

**Add a passing chord**:
Between I and IV, insert a chord whose bass note connects them chromatically.
C → Eb/Bb → F (the Eb/Bb "passes through" on the way to F)

**Deceptive resolution**:
Where the listener expects V→I, go V→vi instead.
Creates emotional surprise — sadness, longing, twist.

**Pedal point**:
Hold the tonic in the bass while changing chords above.
Creates: tension (if non-chord tones are in bass) or unity (drone effect)

**Circle of fifths motion**:
Move through chords whose roots are a 5th apart.
Example: Em → Am → Dm → G → C (moves counterclockwise through circle)
Creates a sense of inevitability, forward motion.

**Modal interchange (borrowing)**:
In a major key, borrow i, iv, ♭VI, ♭VII from parallel minor.
Creates emotional depth, darkness within a generally bright piece.
Example in C major: C → F → fm → C (fm is borrowed from C minor)

## TEXTURE & ARRANGEMENT

### Types of musical texture
- **Monophony**: single melody, no harmony (Gregorian chant, solo flute line)
- **Homophony**: melody + chords supporting it (most pop, hymns)
- **Polyphony**: multiple independent melodic lines simultaneously (Bach fugues)
- **Heterophony**: multiple performers playing same melody with variations

### Arrangement principles

**Register separation**: Low instruments anchor, middle instruments fill harmony,
high instruments carry melody. Don't crowd all voices in the same register.

**Density control**: Not every instrument plays all the time.
Silence and space are compositional elements.

**Countermelody**: A second melodic line that complements (not copies) the main melody.
Should contrast in rhythm, register, and contour with the main melody.

**Call and response**: One instrument/voice "calls", another "responds".
Fundamental to blues, gospel, jazz, African music traditions.

**Rhythmic contrast**: If melody is long and flowing, give it a rhythmic accompaniment.
If melody is rhythmically active, support with long sustained chords.

## VOICE LEADING RULES

Voice leading = how individual voices (soprano, alto, tenor, bass) move from chord to chord.

### The golden rules:
1. **Common tones**: if two adjacent chords share a note, keep it in the same voice (don't move it)
2. **Contrary motion**: when possible, soprano and bass move in opposite directions
3. **Stepwise motion preferred**: voices move by step (2nds) when possible; avoid large leaps
4. **Avoid parallel 5ths and 8ths**: two voices moving in the same direction by a 5th or octave = weak, hollow sound (forbidden in classical counterpoint; fine in rock)
5. **Resolve the leading tone**: the 7th degree always wants to go up to the tonic
6. **Resolve the 7th**: the 7th of a dominant chord always resolves down by step

### Why good voice leading matters:
It's the difference between chord changes that sound smooth and natural vs. clunky.
Compare: C → G with all voices moving by large leaps vs. C → G with voices barely moving.

## COUNTERPOINT BASICS

Counterpoint = the art of combining two or more independent melodic lines.

### First species (note against note)
- One note in the countermelody for every note in the cantus firmus
- Use consonances: unisons, 3rds, 5ths, 6ths, octaves
- Begin and end on unison, 5th, or octave
- Prefer contrary motion, avoid parallel 5ths/octaves

### Second species (2:1 — two notes against one)
- First note of each pair: consonance
- Second note can be a passing tone (dissonance passing by step)

### Practical modern application:
- Write your melody
- Write a bass line that moves mostly contrary to the melody
- Fill in inner voices using voice leading principles
- Result: naturally smooth, professional-sounding arrangement

## GENRE-SPECIFIC COMPOSITION TIPS

### Blues
- Use dominant 7th chords everywhere (even on I and IV)
- Blue notes (♭3, ♭5, ♭7) create the characteristic tension
- Call-and-response structure in phrases
- Shuffle or swing rhythmic feel

### Jazz
- Start with ii-V-I as your harmonic building block
- Add extensions (7ths, 9ths, 13ths) to every chord
- Create interest through harmonic rhythm and substitutions
- Melody often delays resolution, plays over/around chord tones

### Classical (Baroque/Classical period)
- Strict voice leading
- Clear phrase structure (4-bar antecedent → 4-bar consequent)
- Functional harmony (tonal centers clearly established and departed)
- Development through sequences and modulation

### Rock/Pop
- Power of I-IV-V and their variations
- Riff-based: a memorable riff can carry an entire song
- Dynamics as arrangement tool (quiet verse → loud chorus)
- Pentatonic scales for guitar solos and melodies

### Flamenco
- Phrygian mode and Phrygian dominant (1 ♭2 3 4 5 ♭6 ♭7)
- Andalusian cadence: i–VII–VI–V (Am–G–F–E)
- Rhythmic complexity: 12-beat compás in different groupings
- Ornaments: mordents, trills, rapid runs (picado)
