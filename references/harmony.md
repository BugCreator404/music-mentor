## DIATONIC CHORDS

Every major key produces 7 diatonic chords (one per scale degree).

### Chords in a major key (C major example):
```
Degree:  I      ii     iii    IV     V      vi     vii°
Chord:   Cmaj   Dm     Em     Fmaj   G      Am     Bdim
Quality: major  minor  minor  major  major  minor  dim
7th:     Cmaj7  Dm7    Em7    Fmaj7  G7     Am7    Bm7♭5
```

### Functions (what each chord "does"):
| Function | ES | Chords | Character |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tonic | Tónica | I, iii, vi | Stability, home, rest |
| Subdominant | Subdominante | IV, ii | Movement away from home |
| Dominant | Dominante | V, vii° | Tension, wants to resolve to I |

**Key principle**: Music is a tension/resolution cycle.
T → SD → D → T is the fundamental motion of tonal music.

## CADENCES

A cadence is a harmonic ending — how a phrase "breathes" or "lands."

| Cadence | ES | Motion | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Authentic (perfect) | Auténtica perfecta | V→I (both in root position) | Strong, conclusive |
| Authentic (imperfect) | Auténtica imperfecta | V→I (inverted or with 3rd on top) | Conclusive but softer |
| Half cadence | Semicadencia | ?→V | Open, questioning, suspense |
| Plagal | Plagal | IV→I | Gentle, "Amen" feel |
| Deceptive | Rota / Evitada | V→vi | Surprise, emotional twist |
| Phrygian | Frigia | iv6→V | Common in Baroque, flamenco |

## COMMON PROGRESSIONS

### Pop/Rock (in major)
```
I–V–vi–IV       C–G–Am–F     ("Axis progression" — hundreds of songs)
I–IV–V–I        C–F–G–C      (Classic blues/country/folk)
I–vi–IV–V       C–Am–F–G     (50s progression / doo-wop)
vi–IV–I–V       Am–F–C–G     (Minor feel on major key)
I–V–IV–I        C–G–F–C      (Rock standard)
```

### Minor progressions
```
i–VII–VI–VII    Am–G–F–G     (Natural minor, very common)
i–iv–V–i        Am–Dm–E–Am   (Harmonic minor, classical feel)
i–VI–III–VII    Am–F–C–G     (Pop minor)
i–VII–VI–V      Am–G–F–E     (Andalusian cadence — flamenco)
```

### Jazz progressions
```
ii–V–I          Dm7–G7–Cmaj7  (The fundamental jazz motion)
I–vi–ii–V       Cmaj7–Am7–Dm7–G7  (Rhythm changes A section)
I–IV–iii–VI     Cmaj7–Fmaj7–Em7–A7  (Turnaround)
iii–VI–ii–V–I   Em7–A7–Dm7–G7–Cmaj7  (Extended turnaround)
```

### Blues (12-bar)
```
I7  I7  I7  I7
IV7 IV7 I7  I7
V7  IV7 I7  V7   (quick change variation)
```
Note: In blues, even the I chord is dominant (I7), creating constant tension.

## NON-DIATONIC CHORDS

### Secondary dominants
Any dominant 7th chord that resolves to a diatonic chord other than I.
Formula: V7 of [target chord]

Most common:
```
V7/V  =  D7  in C major  →  resolves to G
V7/IV =  C7  in C major  →  resolves to F
V7/ii =  A7  in C major  →  resolves to Dm
V7/vi =  E7  in C major  →  resolves to Am
```
Effect: creates momentary sense of "going somewhere new" before returning to key.

### Borrowed chords (modal mixture)
Chords "borrowed" from the parallel minor (same root, different mode).

In C major, borrowing from C minor:
```
♭VII  = Bb major   (from Mixolydian / minor)  — very common in rock/pop
♭III  = Eb major   (from minor)
♭VI   = Ab major   (from minor)
iv    = Fm         (minor subdominant — emotional, often before I)
♭II   = Db major   (Neapolitan chord — dramatic)
```

### Neapolitan chord (♭II)
A major chord built on the flattened 2nd degree.
In C: Db major
Function: substitutes for IV or ii before V — very dramatic, operatic feel.
Used in: classical music, metal, film scores.

## JAZZ HARMONY ESSENTIALS

### Tritone substitution
Replace a dominant 7th with the dominant 7th a tritone away.
Why it works: both chords share the same tritone (3rd and 7th swap roles).

```
G7 (tritone: B–F)  ↔  Db7 (tritone: F–B)
```
G7→C becomes Db7→C (chromatic bass motion: Db→C)

### Chord extensions in context
- **9th**: adds color, very common (Cmaj9, Dm9, G9)
- **11th**: usually ♯11 on major chords (avoids clash with 3rd), natural 11 on minor/dominant
- **13th**: common on dominant chords (G13 = G B D F A — the A is the 13th)
- **Altered dominants**: ♭9, ♯9, ♭13 on V7 chords → maximum tension → resolution

### ii–V–I analysis
The engine of jazz harmony:
```
Dm7  →  G7  →  Cmaj7
ii7  →  V7  →  Imaj7

Dm7:  subdominant function  (away from home)
G7:   dominant function     (maximum tension, tritone B-F wants to resolve)
Cmaj7: tonic               (resolution, home)
```

## HARMONIC RHYTHM

Not just WHAT chords, but WHEN they change.

- **Slow harmonic rhythm**: chords change every 2-4 bars → peaceful, floating
- **Fast harmonic rhythm**: chords change every beat → tension, forward motion
- **Syncopated changes**: chord changes off the beat → jazz/groove feel

Technique: vary harmonic rhythm within a piece for contrast and drama.

## REHARMONIZATION TECHNIQUES

How to make existing progressions more interesting:

1. **Add 7ths**: C → Cmaj7, G → G7, Am → Am7
2. **Insert secondary dominant**: before any chord, add its V7
   (Am–F becomes A7–Am–F)
3. **Substitute IV for ii**: both are subdominant function, ii is more colorful
4. **Tritone sub**: replace V7 with ♭II7
5. **Modal mixture**: replace I or IV with borrowed chords (i, iv, ♭VII)
6. **Pedal point**: hold bass note while chords change above it

## ANALYSIS WORKFLOW (step-by-step)

When a user gives chords to analyze:

1. **Find the key**: look for the V7 chord (has a leading tone), or identify the
   most stable-sounding chord as I
2. **Assign Roman numerals**: label each chord by scale degree
3. **Mark functions**: T (tonic), SD (subdominant), D (dominant)
4. **Identify non-diatonic chords**: what are they? secondary dominant? borrowed?
5. **Name cadences**: where do phrases end? what cadence type?
6. **Describe the emotional arc**: what does this progression feel like? why?

Example analysis — C G Am F (I–V–vi–IV):
```
C     = I    → Tonic (home, stable)
G     = V    → Dominant (tension — the leading tone B wants to go to C)
Am    = vi   → Tonic substitute (deceptive resolution — surprising but not jarring)
F     = IV   → Subdominant (movement, slight release before returning to C)
Cadence: ends on IV → half cadence feel → keeps looping naturally
Character: hopeful, anthemic, universally appealing — explains why hundreds of songs use it
```
